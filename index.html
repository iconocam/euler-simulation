Fields of Decay

An evolving essay in differential equations and biological systems

Every living system changes. Cells consume energy, memories reorganize, networks grow and sometimes collapse. These processes follow mathematical laws that describe how systems evolve through time. In many natural systems, the rate of change depends on the current state of the system itself. Population growth, radioactive decay, neural activity, and chemical reactions can all be described with differential equations where change is proportional to what already exists. The Euler method offers a simple way to approximate these dynamics. Instead of solving an equation all at once, we move forward through time in small steps, repeatedly updating the system using its current rate of change. Step by step, the future unfolds from the present. Through this lens, we can watch systems evolve: structures form, stabilize, and sometimes disperse as energy spreads and entropy increases. What appears as decay or collapse is often just the natural consequence of systems following the mathematics that governs change.

The Method

TRUST: You don't decide to trust someone completely on day one. You extend a small amount, observe how it's received, extend slightly more or retract slightly — each interaction a step, each step governed by the current state of the relationship. Trust is an Euler integration over time. So is its collapse. The decay term is always present. The question is whether or not the growth term exceeds it.
Given a state y and its rate of change f(t, y), each step advances:

Euler Step
y(t + dt)  =  y(t)  +  dt · f(t, y(t))
y(t + dt) → the next state after the step
y(t) → the current state of the system
dt → a tiny step forward in time
t → time
f(t, y) → how fast the system is changing

The value a moment from now equals the value right now plus a small change

This method is simple and first-order, yet surprisingly powerful. Its approximations introduce small errors, and those errors accumulate as the steps continue. But that accumulation is not just a limitation — it can also be revealing.
CORE IDEA: Euler's method is the mathematics of creatures who cannot see the future but must move through it anyway — which is to say, it is the mathematics of everything alive. In many biological systems, where precision is never perfect and processes drift over time, Euler’s step feels almost honest. It lets us watch change unfold step by step, the way it often does in nature.
